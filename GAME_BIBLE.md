## 1. The Silence

The universe is enormous, old, and quiet.

Every sapient species that ever looked up asked the same question: *where is
everyone?* The answer, it turned out, was not that they didn't exist. It was
that space is so large, and light so slow, and attention so expensive, that
civilizations bloom and die without ever hearing another voice. Historians
call it **the Silence**. Spacers call it the first rule of the void:

> *Nobody finds you until you make yourself findable.*

Every new Sovereign — every player — begins inside the Silence: one planet,
one telescope's reach of darkness, and the statistical certainty that
someone else is out there, building, mining, arming. The game of the early
years is the game every civilization has played: grind, look, listen, and
decide whether being found is an opportunity or a death sentence.

The Silence is broken only by protocol. A **ping** is a tight-beam hail at a
world you can see. It says exactly one thing: *someone is here.* Whether the
receiver **pings back** is the entire history of galactic diplomacy in
miniature. No ping-back, no contact — the universe stays dark and safe.
Ping-back, and a channel opens that cannot be un-opened: two civilizations
now share a story.

## 2. A short history of everything that matters

**The Expansion.** Long before any living memory, the first starfaring
cultures burned outward from their cradles. They built the first
**Stargates** — paired wormhole anchors that made two points one — and for
an age the galaxy was small.

**The Starfall Wars.** Stargates need fuel. Fuel comes from stars. And stars,
the Expansion learned too late, *keep no gauge*. A star harvested past its
limit does not dim politely — it **supernovas**, erasing everything within
its light-hours: fleets, worlds, histories. Whole trade empires vanished in
single afternoons because a rival — or an ally, or a fool — drew one tank
too many. The wars that followed weren't fought over territory. They were
fought over *restraint*.

**The Scattering.** The survivors did the only rational thing: they spread
out, went quiet, and stopped sharing star charts. Stargate networks were cut
or tolled. The galaxy fractured into millions of pocket-civilizations, each
convinced it might be the last. That is the universe you inherit: littered
with dead gates that open onto empty coordinates, wreck fields from battles
nobody remembers, and stars that are — maybe — one harvest away from
Starfall.

**Now.** The Silence is thawing. New Sovereigns are waking cold colonies,
lighting telescopes, and rebuilding the network one tolled route at a time.
Every chat channel opened between two players is, in-fiction, a historic
event: two islands of civilization finding each other across the dark.

## 3. The three peoples

Character NPCs — crews, governors, specialists — come from three peoples.
Any character may be *he*, *she*, or *androgynous*; all three peoples mix
freely aboard ships and in markets.

### Humans
The most numerous and the most varied. Humanity scattered early and adapted
to everything: you will find human dock-rats hauling scrap under a rich
skyline, and human dynasts running planetary exchanges. **Humans span the
whole ladder — visibly poor and visibly rich** — and their markets show it:
patched thermal cloaks queuing beside brocade. Humans are the generalists:
any role, any politics, any price.

### The Forged (robots)
Machine lineages left over from the Expansion's automated fleets, long since
self-governing. A Forged does not eat, sleep, or panic — but it *contracts*:
service is a covenant, and a Forged bound to a ship or a world serves it to
destruction. (This is the lore of **permanent binding**: a Forged fuses its
process into its host; there is no un-fusing, for them or for anyone who
signs the same covenant-rite they invented.) Like humans, the Forged run
poor to rich — a rusting stevedore frame and a gleaming exchange-arbiter are
the same people.

### The Vess (humanoid aliens)
Tall, unhurried, and **always, without exception, rich**. The Vess are the
oldest surviving culture — the only one that came through the Starfall Wars
with its wealth intact, because the Vess never kept their wealth anywhere a
star could burn it. They are the galaxy's memory of compound interest. A
Vess in your market means your market matters; a Vess governor on your world
is a statement of arrival. Nobody has ever seen a poor Vess. The Vess do not
discuss this.

## 4. The Sovereign and the personal ship

You are a **Sovereign**: a consciousness legally and technically anchored to
a **personal ship** — a small, unarmed, unattackable vessel that is less a
vehicle than an *embodiment*. In-fiction, the personal ship's hull carries a
quantum anchor that makes it impossible to target, board, strand, or starve:
destroying it would require deleting you from causality, and the universe
declines.

At awakening (game start) every Sovereign chooses their **politics** — the
governing archetype their anchor was cut to: Militarist, Industrialist,
Mercantile, Scientific, Civic, or Diplomatic. Park your personal ship over a
world and it *governs as you*: your politics apply, a preview of what a
governor of your kind would permit. It is how every Sovereign bootstraps
their first colony before they can afford professionals — and how the wise
ones rehearse a governor appointment before making it permanent.

Because governor appointments **are** permanent. The covenant-rite the
Forged invented became the galaxy's standard: a governor's bond to a world,
a ship's crew to its hull — these are fused, and they share fate. When the
ship burns, the crew's story ends with it. Choose slowly.

## 5. Places

### Planets
Worlds come **small, medium, large**; land is counted in **blocks** (tiles),
and no world has much of it — space is cheap, *ground* is precious. Every
world has a **climate**:

- **Temperate** — the kindest: broad resource menus, easy building.
- **Hot** — close-sun furnaces and venusian pressure-cookers; hard on hulls,
  generous with energetic minerals.
- **Cold** — ice ages and methane snows; slow, stable, rich in cryogenic
  chemistry.
- **Poison** — atmospheres that eat construction alloys. **Nothing can be
  built there — ever** — but their crystal fields are the purest known.
  Poison worlds are mined from orbit and by expendable landers, never
  settled. Every fortune has at least one poison world in it somewhere.

Every world also has a **quality** — the A-to-F ladder assayers use for how
deep its deposits run and how much life it can carry. An **A-class** world
is a legend traded between empires. Most of the sky is **F**: thin dirt over
cold rock, honest and exhaustible.

And they *do* exhaust. Deposits are finite. Mines run dry. Whole regions of
the galaxy are post-extraction husks, and the drive to find, buy, or take
the next world is the engine of everything.

### Stars
Stars are the galaxy's fuel depots — **cold stars, hot stars, gas giants
alight** — each yielding its own fuel chemistry. Harvesting is a dance with
a furnace: the closer you dip, the more you draw and the more your hull
pays. Beyond the **standoff line** you sip safely and slowly. And always,
the harvester's prayer: *may someone else's greed come after mine* — because
no instrument in existence can read how much a star has left, and a star
with nothing left is a **Starfall**: a supernova that erases the neighborhood
without warning or appeal. Where the greatest stars fall, spacetime keeps
the wound open: a **black hole**.

### Black holes
The only polite place in the universe to throw something away. Junk dumped
into open space becomes a drifting hazard field, shredding hulls that cross
it (pirates weaponize this; navies grieve it). Junk fed to a black hole is
simply *gone*. Salvagers hate black holes. Everyone else calls them civic
infrastructure.

### Artificial planets
The endgame of civilization: if the sky won't give you the world you need,
**build one**. An artificial planet is a manufactured world — land-blocks,
districts, population, the works — assembled in empty space at a cost that
beggars empires and a timescale that outlives patience. They hold no ores
and no crystals; they live on imports. But they can be built *anywhere*,
they **move** (slowly, majestically), and they carry **their own Stargate
with them** — which means the network itself can walk. The old powers built
them as capitals. The new ones build them as siege engines of geography.

### The void
Between everything: navigable, empty, honest. Anyone can fly a straight line
to any coordinate — the void doesn't care. It only keeps score: fuel and
life-support, out and back. Run out of fuel and you drift, stranded but
alive, praying someone you trust reads your beacon first. Run out of water,
food, or air and the void takes the crew — after which the hull belongs, by
the oldest law there is, to **whoever comes to get it**.

## 6. Substances

### The twelve (basic materials)
Oxygen, carbon, hydrogen, ore, lithium, sulfur, gold, uranium, deuterium,
aluminium, phosphor, silicon. Every world yields some of the twelve; they
are the alphabet of industry.

### Crystals (advanced, climate-born)
Each climate concentrates its own lattice:
- **Glace** — blue-white, from cold worlds; cryo-chemistry and optics.
- **Ignis** — violet-red, from hot worlds; energetic and volatile.
- **Virid** — green-blue, from temperate worlds; versatile, biocompatible.
- **Nox** — black, from poison worlds only; the purest and the priciest,
  harvested where nothing can live.

### Refined goods
Steel (light, heavy), water (normal, heavy), three foods, three medicines —
the things populations and shipyards actually consume, cooked from the
twelve in dedicated industry.

### Fuel cells — "cells"
Crystals, refined, become **fuel cells**: dense, stable, universal energy.
Cells power industry, districts, stations — *everything except travel* —
and because everyone needs them and any refinery can make them from local
crystal, cells are what the galaxy actually saves in. There is no money in
the universe; there are cells. ("Spice," the old traders called the idea:
the one thing everyone will always take in payment.)

### Propulsion fuel
Travel burns **star-fuel** — cold, hot, or gas, straight from the stars —
and every drive is tuned to a chemistry. A cold-tuned freighter in a
gas-star region is a beached whale. Fleet doctrine begins with a map of
*your* stars.

### Derived items
The rare crafted things — beam lasers, harvest rigs, terraform cores — each
one an individually-forged object with a serial number and a history. A few
exist at a time. Empires have gone to war over a crate of them.

## 7. Society

### Markets
Trade happens where goods physically are: a market sells *that world's*
stock, and a fortune elsewhere is no fortune here. Market squares are the
one place all three peoples crowd together — Vess factors in silk beside
Forged stevedores beside human spacers arguing over crystal grades, with
ship parts and scrap drifting through every scene. Prices float on what the
pair-pools hold; smart marketers get rich on thin pairs; the market takes
its cut on every leg.

### Factions
Any group may **charter a faction** — mint its banner, name its members,
appoint moderators. The banner flies over every member's worlds for all the
galaxy to read. What a faction *demands* of members or neighbors — tribute,
tolls, conduct — is its own law, enforced by its own fleets. The universe
recognizes exactly one thing: the flag. Everything else is politics, and
politics is players.

### The wager-houses (casinos)
Where there is wealth there is the wish to risk it. Casino districts are
lore-honest: glittering, Vess-frequented, and where more than one empire's
succession was decided over cards. (Mechanically: commerce districts with
personality — see the Design Guide.)

### Recruitment
Skilled characters aren't manufactured; they're *found* — via **recruitment
pods**: cryo-cached specialists from the Scattering, bought sight-unseen for
whatever resource the recruiters' guild currently finds scarce. (The guild's
pricing is merciless: the more common your offered resource is
galaxy-wide, the more of it they demand.) Open the pod, meet your new pilot
— or, on the rarest days, a governor worth a world.

## 8. The two deaths, salvage, and wrecks

Spacers distinguish the **cold death** (fuel out: drifting, alive,
recoverable, still *yours*) from the **long death** (life-support out: crew
gone, covenant dissolved, hull ownerless). The long death feeds the
salvage economy: recyclers with claim-rigs prowl old battlefields and dead
gate-exits, collecting junk fields and claiming ownerless hulls. Combat
*creates* geography — every battle leaves a debris field that reroutes
traffic for years. Pirates camp dead Stargate exits. Salvagers follow
pirates. Navies follow salvagers. That's a trade route now.

## 9. Themes and tone

- **Distance is the antagonist.** Not evil empires — *light-lag, fuel math,
  and the price of being far away.* Every mechanic bends around distance.
- **Restraint is strategy.** Overcrowd a world, overdraw a star, overfill a
  warehouse — the universe's answer is always the same tilted bell curve:
  past the sweet spot, everything you add makes it worse.
- **Permanence has teeth.** Governors don't resign. Stars don't refill.
  Deposits don't regrow. Choices age with you.
- **Politics is player-made.** The game supplies levers — pings, tolls,
  flags, escorts, markets. It never supplies morals.
- **It is a game first.** No currency, no yield-farming, no pay-to-win: you
  can buy a *place to stand*, never the strength to win. (#P2Enjoy.)

**Visual key:** old-school isometric; deep blacks and dark purples cut by
vivid yellows; colorful, bright, alive. Ships and stations are silhouettes
you learn to read at a glance — every upgrade visible, bolted on top of the
hull you already know.
